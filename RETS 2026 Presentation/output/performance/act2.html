⚠ Before You Go All In

Three things
that went wrong.

None of this is theoretical. All of it has already happened.

Cautionary Tale

Zillow trusted
the algorithm too much.

$500M+ lost
Zillow Offers used its Zestimate AI to buy homes instantly. Q3 2021: bought 9,680 homes, sold only 3,032. The algorithm — trained on a rising market — had no idea the market was about to turn. Result: $304M write-down in a single quarter. 2,000 people fired. $7.8B in market cap erased.

CEO Rich Barton: "The unpredictability in forecasting home prices far exceeds what we anticipated."

The lesson: AI models trained on rising markets break when markets shift. The algorithm wasn't lying. It just didn't know what it didn't know.
Cautionary Tale

AI trained on
biased data gives
biased results.

$48,000
Homes in Black neighbourhoods are undervalued by an average of $48,000 per property — representing $156 billion in cumulative losses nationally (Brookings, 2018).

In one documented case: a home was appraised at $995,000. The owner removed evidence of race and had a white friend present instead. It was reappraised at $1,482,500 — nearly $500,000 higher. The algorithm reproduced the discrimination embedded in its training data.

For Zimbabwe: AI valuations trained on limited local data may systematically under- or over-value specific areas. This isn't theoretical — it's the documented pattern wherever AI valuations have deployed without monitoring.
Cautionary Tale

When the listing
is better than the house.

2.6M views
A Detroit listing generated 2.6 million views with AI-enhanced photos of a beautifully renovated bungalow. The actual property was in severe disrepair. In Minnesota, an agent found AI had added a nonexistent window to a bedroom photo.

The Atlantic (Feb 2026): "The Unsettling Rise of AI Real-Estate Slop." A photographer estimated at least 12 of every 100 listings he reviewed had been materially enhanced or entirely fabricated. Hallucinated staircases. Missing kitchen cabinets. Rooms that connect to nowhere.

Australia: $5,500–$22,000 fines for undisclosed AI-altered images. California AB 723 (2026): misdemeanour charges. Your reputation is the one thing that can't be automated — until it's gone.
The Uncomfortable Truth

None of what Agent Jon did
was actually illegal.

The targeting? Legal. The photo removal? Technically legal. The firing? Legal.
RealPage priced 4 million apartments through an algorithm — DOJ had to invent a new legal theory to stop it.

AI doesn't have intent. It optimises. The harm — when there is harm — is an emergent property of optimisation applied at scale to an imperfect world.

The question isn't "is this AI lying?"
It's "what is this AI optimising for — and what gets distorted in service of that?"
