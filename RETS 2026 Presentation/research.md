# AI in real estate: the complete briefing for RETS 2026

**AI is transforming real estate globally at breakneck speed — and Zimbabwe stands at a unique inflection point to leapfrog decades of Western PropTech evolution.** The global AI-in-real-estate market has exploded from $2.9 billion in 2024 to a trajectory of $41.5 billion by 2033,  growing at 30.5% annually.  In the U.S., **82% of real estate agents now use AI tools**,  up from near-zero just three years ago. Yet across Africa, adoption remains nascent — creating both a widening gap and a once-in-a-generation opportunity. Zimbabwe, with 91.6% mobile penetration  but only 38.4% internet access,  mirrors the exact conditions that gave birth to M-Pesa’s mobile banking revolution in Kenya. The question for RETS 2026 attendees isn’t whether AI will reshape Zimbabwe’s property market — it’s who will build the tools that do it.

## The global AI real estate revolution is already here

The numbers tell a dramatic story of acceleration. Morgan Stanley projects AI will deliver **$34 billion in efficiency gains** for real estate by 2030,  with the capacity to automate **37% of all tasks** across the industry.  PropTech venture capital hit $16.7 billion in 2025 — a 67.9% year-over-year increase  — and AI-centered PropTech investment is growing at nearly double the rate of non-AI companies (42% vs. 24% annualized). 

The practical applications are already reshaping how properties are bought, sold, and managed worldwide. Zillow’s neural-network Zestimate now values **116 million U.S. homes**  with an on-market error rate of just **1.83%**  — meaning for a $500,000 home, the algorithm is typically accurate to within $9,150.  Compass, America’s largest residential brokerage by volume, has invested **$1.5 billion in technology** over the past decade and deployed a “Likely to Sell” AI tool where roughly 8% of monthly recommendations result in listings within 12 months. EliseAI, valued at $2.2 billion,  now powers **1 in 8 apartments in the United States**, handling tenant communications, lease renewals, and maintenance requests while cutting administrative costs by up to 25%. 

The agent-level adoption data from NAR’s 2025 Technology Survey  (1,200+ respondents) reveals a profession in rapid transition: **68% of Realtors use AI-generated content**,  with 1 in 5 using AI daily.  The most impactful use case? Writing listing descriptions — **68.47%** of agents say AI delivers its greatest impact there. Writing tools are the number-one AI tool category at 77.93% usage, followed by chatbots (47%), image editing (39%), and market analysis (39%). AI virtual staging increases property inquiries by up to **200%**  while reducing traditional staging costs by **97%**. Properties using AI staging and generative content go live **73% faster** and sell for **25% higher prices**. 

On the investor side, JLL’s 2025 Global Survey found **88% of real estate investors have started piloting AI**  — up from just 5% in 2023 — pursuing an average of 5 AI use cases simultaneously.  Yet over 60% remain strategically unprepared for scaling beyond pilots,   suggesting the gap between early adopters and laggards is widening fast.

## Africa’s PropTech landscape is emerging but faces a fundamental data challenge

South Africa leads the continent in real estate AI maturity. Lightstone Property pioneered an **AI-powered valuation model in 2019** using neural networks and gradient boosting to estimate values across **7.1 million South African residential properties**. It became the first African and only non-European member of the European AVM Alliance — an internationally recognized validation of its AI valuations. Its data feeds into lending decisions at most leading South African banks. Property24, the country’s largest portal  with **430,000+ listings and 6 million monthly visitors**, provides automated valuations. Newer entrants like FindHomes (Cape Town’s first AI-powered property portal using natural language search)  and Flow Living (AI-automated marketing for agencies)  are pushing the frontier further.

Zimbabwe’s real estate market, estimated at **US$119 billion in 2024**  with the residential segment projected to grow to $104.1 billion by 2029,  is digitizing rapidly but from a lower base. Propertybook.co.zw  — the host of RETS 2026 — has grown into Zimbabwe’s largest property portal with **300,000+ monthly visitors**,  over **8,500 active listings** across 350 neighborhoods, and **2,000+ registered agents** covering 80+ cities. It has already deployed AI content engines for SEO and property descriptions, representing one of the earliest practical AI applications in Zimbabwe’s property sector. Property.co.zw (7,730+ properties) and Zimbabwe Property Connect provide additional digital infrastructure.  Pam Golding Zimbabwe operates within the broader Pam Golding network, which acquired digital agency Eazi.com in 2018 and formed a joint venture with BidX1 for digital auctions. 

Across the continent, Egypt’s Nawy has emerged as Africa’s PropTech champion — raising **$75 million** in 2025  (Africa’s largest-ever PropTech round) and growing from $38 million to **$1.4 billion in GMV** in just four years. The biggest barrier is data. Academic research published in 2025 concluded Africa’s real estate market is **“yet to be ready for adoption of hi-tech market analysis devices such as AI”** due to insufficient reliable data. Western AVMs depend on millions of comparable transactions; most African markets lack centralized property databases. In South Africa, even with Lightstone’s advanced systems, a single misclassified transfer can skew AI models by **20-30% in valuation accuracy**. Six structural constraints were identified: knowledge gaps, weak political will, unstable economic conditions, low market transparency, lack of data availability, and insufficient infrastructure. 

Zimbabwe faces particular challenges. Only **38.4% of the population** has internet access (6.45 million users out of 16.8 million). The ZiG currency’s sharp depreciation has undermined trust, with real estate transactions overwhelmingly conducted in US dollars.  Inflation hit **51% in 2024**.  Only 65% have 4G access, and 5G remains limited to Harare and Bulawayo.  Yet **87%+ access the internet via mobile devices**, and mobile penetration stands at 91.6%  — creating the same mobile-dominant conditions that enabled revolutionary tech adoption elsewhere in Africa.

## The West is 7-10 years ahead, but Africa can skip entire generations of technology

The gap between Western and African real estate AI adoption is substantial but not insurmountable. Western PropTech investment is roughly **40-50x greater** than Africa’s. The entire African startup ecosystem raised $1.4 billion in H1 2025; U.S. PropTech M&A alone generated $2.3 billion in the same period.  While 75% of U.S. brokerages use AI tools,  African adoption remains in early pilot stages. The U.S. has comprehensive MLS databases, standardized property records, and decades of transaction data. Most of Sub-Saharan Africa relies on word-of-mouth, paper-based land records, and informal networks, with a housing deficit of **51+ million units**.

What the West has that Africa doesn’t — yet — tells the story:

- **Automated valuations at scale**: Zillow covers 136M+ properties;  HouseCanary processes 1,000+ data points per property with 2.8% median error. Africa has no comparable infrastructure outside South Africa.
- **AI-powered mortgage processing**: RXR Realty’s AI fraud screening reduced delinquency from 4.16% to 0.02%.  In most African markets, mortgages are “almost non-existent” (Nawy’s CEO).
- **Virtual tours as standard**: The VR real estate market is projected at $80 billion by 2025 globally.  In Africa, 3D tours remain a novelty.
- **Smart contracts and digital closings**: Bergen County, New Jersey migrated 370,000 property deeds worth $240 billion onto blockchain, slashing settlement from 90 days to 1. Africa is experimenting but nowhere near this scale.

The M-Pesa analogy is the most powerful frame for RETS 2026. When M-Pesa launched in Kenya in 2007, there were only **5 bank branches per 100,000 people** in Sub-Saharan Africa — but mobile phone penetration was 73%. Within years, M-Pesa had **31.8 million active users**, processed 11 billion transactions annually, and **25% of Kenya’s GDP** flowed through it. An MIT study found it lifted **200,000 households out of poverty**. Today, Africa has **~400 million registered mobile money accounts** — nearly half the global total. Safaricom Ethiopia is now achieving in **18 months what took 18 years in Kenya**.

Real estate AI meets all three criteria from the Science journal’s 2025 framework for successful technology leapfrogging: the technology is already proven in mature markets; it offers a workaround to expensive, obsolete infrastructure; and it can provide superior functionality for users compared to existing processes. The specific leapfrog opportunities are compelling:

- **WhatsApp AI property agents**: With **320 million WhatsApp users in Africa** (97% adoption in Kenya, 96% in South Africa, 95% in Nigeria) and a 98% message open rate,   WhatsApp-based AI property concierges could bypass the need for websites, apps, or MLS systems entirely. India’s NoBroker used WhatsApp to cut property photo upload time from **3 days to 6 minutes**, increasing sold/rented properties 2.8x.
- **Satellite imagery + AI valuations**: Freely available satellite data combined with mobile photos and crowdsourced market data could generate property valuations without traditional comparable sales databases.
- **Mobile money closings**: M-Pesa rails are already in place for instant, secure property payment transactions.
- **Voice-first multilingual search**: AI that understands Shona, Ndebele, or other local languages could make property search accessible to populations that current text-based platforms cannot serve.

Africa’s structural advantages are real. The continent’s median age is **~19 years** (versus 38 in the U.S. and 40 in Europe), with 830 million young Africans entering the workforce by 2050. There’s no legacy infrastructure to replace — no entrenched MLS systems, no decades-old CRMs. Urban population will **double from 700 million to 1.4 billion by 2050**,  creating unprecedented demand. And mobile-first culture means solutions can be designed for smartphones from day one rather than adapted from desktop platforms.

## Cautionary tales every Zimbabwe agent should know

The most expensive AI failure in real estate history belongs to Zillow. Its iBuying arm, Zillow Offers, used the Zestimate algorithm to make instant cash offers on homes — and it went catastrophically wrong. In Q3 2021, Zillow purchased **9,680 homes but sold only 3,032**,   as the algorithm consistently overvalued properties while the market cooled. The result: a **$304 million write-down** in a single quarter,  total losses estimated at **$500 million to over $1 billion**,   2,000 employees laid off (25% of the workforce),  and **$7.8 billion in market value** erased.  CEO Rich Barton admitted: “The unpredictability in forecasting home prices far exceeds what we anticipated.”   The lesson is clear — AI models trained on rising markets cannot adapt to sudden shifts.

Zillow wasn’t alone. Opendoor posted a **$1.4 billion net loss** in 2022,  with its stock collapsing 97% from 2021 highs.  Redfin shuttered its iBuying arm after a $22 million quarterly loss.  Reali — which had raised $290 million including a $100 million Series B just one year prior — simply shut down in September 2022.  REX Real Estate, the AI-driven discount brokerage that raised $145 million and bypassed the MLS, ceased operations in May 2022   after discovering that technology alone couldn’t disrupt deeply entrenched industry structures.

Algorithmic bias represents perhaps the most serious ethical risk. The landmark 2018 Brookings study found homes in Black neighborhoods are undervalued by **$48,000 per home on average**, representing **$156 billion in cumulative losses** nationally.   A 2021 Freddie Mac study confirmed Black and Latino applicants are **twice as likely** to receive appraisals below contract price.  The most visceral cases involve “whitewashing” — where homeowners remove evidence of their race to get accurate appraisals. In Marin City, California, Paul and Tenisha Tate-Austin’s home was appraised at $995,000;  after having a white friend present as the homeowner, it was reappraised at **$1,482,500 — nearly $500,000 higher**.   In Baltimore, Johns Hopkins professors Nathan Connolly and Shani Mott saw their home jump from $472,000 to $750,000  — a **59% increase** — after whitewashing. 

For Zimbabwe, where property data is limited and cultural biases may differ from Western contexts, these cases serve as a critical warning: **AI trained on biased data will produce biased results**. Any AI valuation tool deployed in Zimbabwe must be carefully monitored for systematic under- or over-valuation of properties in specific areas or owned by specific groups.

AI-generated content is creating new risks in real estate listings. A viral Detroit listing showed AI-enhanced photos of a pristine, beautifully renovated 1922 bungalow that garnered **2.6 million views** — but the actual property was in severe disrepair.  In Minnesota, an agent touring a home discovered AI had **added a nonexistent window** in a bedroom photo.  Common AI photo failures include staircases that lead nowhere, exaggerated room sizes,  impossible furniture arrangements, and neighboring buildings digitally deleted.  New South Wales, Australia now requires disclosure of AI-altered rental images with fines of **$5,500 to $22,000**.  New York issued a 2025 warning about the “significant rise in artificially generated pictures.” 

Deepfake fraud is escalating, with attacks increasing **40% year-over-year**  and causing over **$200 million in financial losses** in Q1 2025 alone.  In one case, a scammer created a deepfake video of a property owner authorizing a wire transfer, stealing hundreds of thousands of dollars.  The FBI warned of a **500% spike** in vacant land deed fraud from 2020-2024,  with AI increasingly used to forge documents.

## What comes next: agentic AI, tokenized property, and the augmented agent

The most transformative near-term development is **agentic AI** — systems that don’t just generate content but plan, execute, and learn from multi-step workflows autonomously.  McKinsey’s landmark March 2026 analysis estimates agentic AI could unlock **$430-$550 billion in annual value** globally across real estate, construction, and development.  The framework identifies four high-value domains: maintenance/facilities, leasing/renewals, investing/asset management, and construction.  Walmart’s pilot offers a striking preview: agentic AI negotiated **64% of vendor agreements** (target was 20%), achieving 1.5% cost savings — and **83% of vendors preferred dealing with AI over human buyers**.

Blockchain convergence with AI is accelerating. Deloitte projects **$4 trillion of real estate will be tokenized by 2035**. Bergen County, New Jersey migrated 370,000 property deeds onto the Avalanche blockchain, collapsing settlement from 90 days to 1 day. RealT tokenized over **$150 million in multifamily units** in 2025 with daily stablecoin dividends.  For Africa, this convergence has particular significance: Seso Global built Africa’s **first blockchain-based property register**  in Khayelitsha, South Africa, covering approximately 1,000 properties.  Blockchain land registries directly address the continent’s most fundamental real estate challenge  — establishing clear, tamper-proof property rights  where paper records are unreliable or nonexistent. 

Computer vision is transforming property inspection. Cotality’s Property Vision platform uses drones and AI to conduct remote property assessments.  Digital twins — AI-powered virtual replicas of buildings — are going mainstream,  with CoStar’s acquisition of Matterport in 2025 signaling the technology’s maturation.  CapitaLand in Singapore reported a **16.4% reduction in annual capital expenditure** using digital twins.  By 2030, Gartner projects most asset-level decisions will be driven by integrated AI loops combining sensor data, tenant communication, and market intelligence. 

The future agent isn’t replaced by AI — they’re augmented by it. JLL’s survey found **90.1% of companies** expect to carry out real estate activities with AI supporting human experts over the next five years.   McKinsey’s framework draws the line clearly: “automate steps aggressively; protect thoughts deliberately.”  Repeatable tasks get automated; judgment calls, trust-building, and exception-handling stay human. By 2028-2030, an estimated **60-80% of agent tasks** will be automated, particularly affecting the 70%+ of U.S. agents who didn’t close a transaction in 2024. But agents who master AI tools — the “augmented agents” — will handle more transactions, serve clients better, and earn more.

For Zimbabwe and Africa specifically, the critical prediction is timing. **2026-2027 represents the adoption window.** Africa’s urban population doubling to 1.4 billion by 2050,  combined with the $51+ million housing unit deficit, creates unprecedented demand. PropTech accounted for **33% of African tech investment volume** in early 2025.   Nawy’s $75 million raise proves international investors see African PropTech as a serious opportunity.  PropertyPro already operates across Nigeria, Kenya, Zimbabwe, and Uganda.  Egypt’s national AI strategy aims to train 30,000 AI professionals by 2030. Rwanda has achieved 95% 4G coverage. The infrastructure is being built now.

## The gray zone: AI uses that are technically defensible but make people uncomfortable

These are not horror stories. They are cases where reasonable people genuinely disagree — where AI is doing something technically legal, even arguably beneficial, but that crosses a line that's hard to articulate. The line between optimization and manipulation. The line between efficient and predatory. This is the space where the most interesting conversations happen.

---

### 1. AI photo enhancement: the question your tool asks

The most immediate gray-area example is the one Jon built: an AI tool that enhances listing photos — changing angles, improving lighting, optimizing composition. Not fabricating rooms that don't exist. Not hiding structural damage. Just... making the property look its best. Like makeup. Like a haircut before a job interview.

But where is the line? Blue sky replacing an overcast sky: acceptable. Virtual staging showing a furnished room when it's empty: now standard practice (and disclosed in most MLSs). Removing a neighboring building that blocks light: uncomfortable. Adding a lawn to a dirt lot: misrepresentation.

No one has drawn a clear line. **California AB 723** (effective January 1, 2026) attempts to — requiring disclosure of any image "digitally altered" beyond standard color correction, with violators subject to misdemeanor charges. The law is deliberately vague on where "enhancement" ends and "misrepresentation" begins. The legislation explicitly distinguishes "standard photo editing (white balance, exposure adjustments)" from "alterations that change the representation of real property" — but offers no test for which is which.

In 2024, **34% of estate agents faced MLS compliance penalties** related to image issues, with average fines reaching $12,000 and associated reputation damage estimated at $35,000 in lost business. The market for AI image enhancement tools in real estate is growing regardless.

The question isn't whether AI enhancement is wrong. It's: **what exactly is the problem?** Is it lying? Or is it simply presenting a property as well as it can be presented? Traditional staging — renting furniture, hiring a stager, deep-cleaning — does the same thing without AI. The discomfort isn't about deception. It's about what "authentic" means in a world where every layer of presentation is optimizable.

### 2. RealPage: when AI "optimizes" and accidentally fixes prices

RealPage's YieldStar software didn't tell landlords to collude. It just... gave them all the same recommendations. The algorithm ingested nonpublic data from thousands of competing apartment buildings — vacancy rates, lease terms, tenant turnover — and output a single rent recommendation. Each landlord believed they were optimizing independently. Together, they created a cartel without a phone call or a handshake.

The scale is staggering: the software priced **more than 4 million rental units** — roughly 8% of all U.S. rentals. In a Seattle ZIP code analyzed by ProPublica, rent in a RealPage-priced building rose **33% in one year** while a non-algorithm building in the same ZIP code rose 3.9%. The White House estimated algorithmic rent-setting cost renters an average of **$70 per month** in buildings using the software.

The DOJ sued RealPage in 2024. A settlement was reached in November 2025: RealPage agreed to stop using nonpublic competitor data, appointed a monitor for three years, and became a government cooperator against the landlords who used the tool. RealPage immediately turned around and **sued New York State** for banning the software outright.

The philosophical problem: **RealPage's defense is that it was simply providing market information.** Each landlord had a right to know what competitors were charging. Pricing to market is not illegal. The collusion, if it happened, was an emergent property of the system — not anyone's stated intent. As one antitrust attorney noted, the DOJ settlement shows the government is "not treating algorithmic pricing as inherently illegal" — just this specific implementation of it.

This is exactly the optimization-vs-manipulation question applied to the rental market. And it's coming to Zimbabwe: the same logic applies to any AI that recommends listing prices based on what comparable agents are charging.

### 3. AI tenant screening: optimizing for risk vs. encoding discrimination

SafeRent's algorithm was designed to predict which tenants would pay their rent. Reasonable goal. Its SafeRent Score incorporated credit history and certain categories of non-tenancy-related debt — rational inputs for a risk model. The problem: these factors disproportionately disadvantaged Black and Hispanic applicants, and the model specifically failed to account for the fact that housing voucher holders (HUD subsidies) had their rent guaranteed by the government — making them, by definition, lower risk than the model treated them.

A $2.28 million class-action settlement was reached. Under the five-year agreement, SafeRent can no longer display a tenant screening score for housing voucher applicants, nor can it show "accept" or "deny" recommendations for them. The DOJ and HUD filed a Statement of Interest confirming that **"the Fair Housing Act applies to algorithm-based tenant screening, even when discrimination is not intentional."**

The gray area: the algorithm wasn't trying to discriminate. It was trying to predict risk as accurately as possible given available data. The discrimination was an emergent property of historical inequality encoded in credit histories. If you built the algorithm again from scratch with different inputs, you might avoid the disparate impact — but would you be predicting risk less accurately, or more accurately? The answer isn't obvious.

This is the AI fairness paradox: **the most statistically accurate model might be the most discriminatory one.** There is no neutral position. Choosing which inputs to include is itself a values decision.

### 4. AI "life event" targeting: when personalization becomes predation

The real estate industry has a name for the people most likely to sell quickly and on unfavorable terms: **motivated sellers**. The framework is sometimes called the "5 Ds" — Divorce, Debt, Death, Diapers (new baby requiring more space), and Diamonds (marriage). Agents have always known to look for probate listings, divorce filings, pre-foreclosure notices. This is standard practice.

AI has industrialized it. Modern lead-generation platforms now ingest public records (court filings, tax delinquencies, probate filings), social signals, and behavioral data to identify homeowners experiencing these exact life events — and serve them targeted advertising at the precise moment of vulnerability. Compass's "Likely to Sell" tool uses 12+ data signals including length of ownership, local market velocity, and behavioral data to rank contacts by sale probability; **~8% of monthly recommendations result in listings within 12 months**.

What's changed with AI is the scale, precision, and automation of what was previously relationship-based. An agent who knew a family in the neighborhood, knew they'd recently lost a parent, and reached out to offer help — that was considered good customer service. AI that identifies 50,000 recently-bereaved homeowners across a metro area and auto-sends them a listing pitch is the same action at industrial scale. Is it predatory? Or is it efficient market discovery that actually helps people who need to sell find buyers faster?

No law prohibits it. No regulation currently governs it. The discomfort is real and widely reported, but the market argument — that motivated sellers benefit from faster matches — is also coherent.

### 5. AI "slop" listings: when optimization destroys information

By late 2025, a new category of AI problem had emerged in real estate: **AI slop**. Not deliberate deepfakes. Not malicious fraud. Just AI-generated content so generic, so optimized for engagement, so devoid of specific truth, that it had become actively misleading.

A freelance real estate photographer estimated in October 2025 that **at least 12 of every 100 listings** he reviewed had been materially enhanced or entirely fabricated with AI. Agents were generating AI walkthrough videos of homes — with hallucinated staircases, missing kitchen cabinets, rooms that don't connect to anywhere, furniture floating impossibly. The Atlantic called it "The Unsettling Rise of AI Real-Estate Slop" (February 2026). Marketplace reported buyers showing up to viewings having fallen in love with an AI-generated version of a property that bore little relation to what they found.

The deeper problem: this isn't fraud in the traditional sense. The agents using these tools often believe they're just showing the property "at its potential." The AI isn't lying — it's imagining. And in a market where buyers expect heavily curated listing photography, a genuinely accurate raw photo might actually disadvantage a seller. The slop is a market equilibrium: everyone does it because not doing it makes you look worse by comparison, even if your property is identical.

NAR's 2025 Technology Survey found **46% of Realtors now use AI-generated content** for listings. The quality floor has dropped. And no one is sure how to set a standard when the technology is improving faster than regulators can define "accurate."

### 6. AI mortgage underwriting: the fairness paradox at scale

In July 2025, the Massachusetts Attorney General reached a **$2.5 million settlement** with a lender whose AI underwriting model produced statistically significant disparate impact against applicants based on race and immigration status — without the lender having intended any discrimination whatsoever. The model had been trained on historical loan performance data. Historical loan performance data reflects historical lending discrimination. The AI reproduced the past.

The CFPB stated in August 2024: **"There are no exceptions to federal consumer financial protection laws for new technologies."** An algorithmic decision that produces disparate racial outcomes is discriminatory under existing law — regardless of whether anyone intended discrimination.

The Trump administration has since proposed rules that would gut this framework, requiring proof of *intentional* discrimination and eliminating disparate impact liability entirely. The legal future is genuinely uncertain.

But the philosophical question is permanent: **if AI trained on real-world data reproduces real-world inequalities, is that bias or accuracy?** The historical loan data shows that certain groups defaulted at higher rates — not because of any innate characteristic, but because of redlining, discriminatory terms, and limited economic opportunity. An AI model that accounts for this is "accurate." It is also discriminatory. You cannot separate the two without deciding what kind of fairness you want to optimize for.

### The common thread

What connects all six cases is the same tension: **AI doesn't have intent.** It optimizes. It predicts. It pattern-matches. The "harm" — if it is harm — is often an emergent property of optimization applied at scale to an imperfect world.

The question for every agent in Zimbabwe adopting these tools isn't "is this AI lying?" It's "what is this AI optimizing for, and what gets distorted in service of that optimization?" That question doesn't have a regulatory answer yet. It only has an ethical one.

## Conclusion: the presentation thesis in data

The core narrative for Jon Dallas’s RETS 2026 talk is supported by a powerful convergence of evidence. **AI is not future technology for real estate — it is present technology delivering measurable results today.** The 82% adoption rate among U.S. agents,  the 23% increase in closed transactions for AI-using agents,  the 200% increase in listing inquiries from AI staging,  and the 70-90% time savings in document processing are not projections — they are current realities.

Zimbabwe’s unique position — high mobile penetration (91.6%),  growing digital payments, a $119 billion real estate market, and Propertybook’s 300,000 monthly visitors  providing a data foundation — creates conditions ripe for the same kind of technology leapfrogging that made Kenya a global leader in mobile payments.
