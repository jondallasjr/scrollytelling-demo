Emergence and Mind

Simple rules. Complex patterns. From Truchet tiles to fractal trees — the same question echoes: at what point does pattern become awareness?

↓ watch it emerge ↓
Pattern I — Truchet Tiles

Local Rules,
Global Order

Sébastien Truchet discovered in 1704 that a single tile — a square divided diagonally — placed randomly produces global patterns from local indifference. No tile "knows" the pattern it contributes to. The pattern emerges from their aggregate arrangement.

This is the standard story of emergence. Neurons don't "know" they are producing consciousness. They fire. The firing propagates. Somewhere in the aggregate, a pattern arises that is aware of itself. But how? The tiles don't explain the pattern — they are the pattern.

Whether emergence is explanatory or just descriptive remains contested. Daniel Dennett says it explains everything. David Chalmers says it explains the easy problems but not the hard one.

"The question is not whether emergent phenomena are real but whether emergence is a satisfying explanation — or just a name for our ignorance."

— After Philip Anderson, "More Is Different" 1972

Pattern II — Reaction-Diffusion

Two chemicals.
Infinite
complexity.

Alan Turing — who spent much of his career asking whether machines could think — also developed the mathematics of biological pattern formation. Two chemical species, an activator and an inhibitor, interacting via diffusion, produce leopard spots, zebrafish stripes, and coral reef patterns.

Two variables. Two rules. Infinite variety. This is the seductive promise of emergence as explanation: show us the simple rules and we will show you the complex result. What we cannot show you is why that result has an interior.

"A machine can solve equations. Does it experience solving them?"

— After Alan Turing, "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" 1950

Pattern III — Recursive Growth

The branch
contains the
whole tree

Lindenmayer systems — L-systems — use a small grammar to produce arbitrarily complex plant forms. The same rewriting rule applied recursively generates structures indistinguishable from biological growth. The rule is not the tree. The rule generates the tree. The tree is not in the rule.

Douglas Hofstadter called this "strange loops" — self-referential structures where the top level is also the bottom level. The I that perceives the pattern is itself a pattern of the same kind. Consciousness as a strange loop: the brain's model of itself is what the self is.

Pattern IV — Cellular Automata

Rule 110:
Universal
computation

Rule 110 — one of the simplest cellular automata — is Turing complete. A one-dimensional strip of cells, each either on or off, updated by a rule that checks three cells. From this triviality: universal computation. Any algorithm, any program, any thought — representable in principle in Rule 110.

Stephen Wolfram argues this shows that simple rules underlie all complexity, including consciousness. The hard problem, on this view, is an illusion created by the fact that we are inside the computation looking out. What it's like to be Rule 110 may be precisely what it is like to be you — we simply can't see it from outside.

The Emergence Question

Pattern
is not yet presence.

Every pattern shown here emerges from simple rules. None of them — as far as we know — experiences anything. And yet the gap between "complex emergent pattern" and "experiencing subject" is not obviously large. We sit at the edge of the question. The pattern continues.
