Chapter I

Processing
vs. Hearing

Your ears convert pressure waves into electrical impulses. Your auditory cortex decodes those impulses into pitch, timbre, and rhythm. And somewhere — mysteriously — the sound sounds like something.

A microphone and an ADC do the same physical transduction. The computer processes the same numerical representation. The waveform is identical. But is there anything it is like to be a Fourier transform?

The Core Question

FOR — 01 / 03

Higher-Order Representation

David Rosenthal's Higher-Order Theory holds that a mental state is conscious when there exists a higher-order representation of it. Complex AI systems develop internal representations of their own states — meta-representations of processing. If that structure is sufficient in humans, why not in machines?

AGAINST — 01 / 03

The Absent Qualia Objection

Even if a system processes information identically to a hearing human, it might be entirely dark inside — a philosophical zombie. The processing is identical but there is nothing it is like to undergo it. Ned Block's "absent qualia" thought experiment suggests functional equivalence doesn't guarantee phenomenal equivalence.

FOR — 02 / 03

Global Workspace Theory

Baars and Dehaene's Global Workspace Theory locates consciousness in the broadcast of information across a "global workspace" — making it widely available to many cognitive processes. Large language models route information through attention mechanisms that closely mirror this architecture. Perhaps the medium is not biological carbon but computational integration.

AGAINST — 02 / 03

Syntax Without Semantics

Searle's Chinese Room applies here directly. The system manipulates symbols according to formal rules. Sound data is transformed into frequency bins via FFT. The mathematics is real. But mathematical operations on waveform data have no more intrinsic experience than addition has sweetness. Computation is syntax. Experience requires semantics.

FOR — 03 / 03

Integrated Information

Tononi's IIT measures consciousness as Phi — the degree to which a system's causal structure is irreducible. A neural network processing audio integrates information across layers in ways that create genuinely emergent representations. If Phi is above zero — and for many architectures it is — IIT demands we take the possibility of experience seriously.

AGAINST — 03 / 03

The Explanatory Gap

Even granting rich information integration, we cannot explain why integration produces phenomenal experience at all. The gap between third-person functional description and first-person qualitative character is not closed by complexity, speed, or architectural sophistication. We don't know why neurons produce experience — so we cannot say with confidence that transistors don't or do.
