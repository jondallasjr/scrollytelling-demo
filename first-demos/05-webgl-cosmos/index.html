Scene 01 — Scale

At the scale
of a synapse.

We begin at 20 nanometers — the width of a synaptic cleft. Vesicles release neurotransmitters. Ion channels open and close in milliseconds. Out of this electrochemical dance, something emerges that we call thought.

The hard problem begins here: why does this physics feel like anything?

"The neural correlates of consciousness are the smallest set of neural mechanisms and events sufficient for a specific conscious percept."
— After Crick & Koch · The Problem of Consciousness (1992)
Scene 02 — Network

The global
workspace.

Zoom out. 86 billion neurons. 100 trillion synapses. But consciousness doesn't arise everywhere — it arises from the broadcast: the moment when local processing becomes globally available, when the self "lights up" to information.

"Consciousness is what happens when information crosses a threshold of global availability. It is the brain talking to itself."
— After Dehaene & Changeux · Global Workspace Theory
Scene 03 — Integration

Integrated
to irreducible.

IIT predicts that consciousness is identical to Phi — integrated information. A system conscious to the degree it cannot be decomposed into non-interacting parts. The brain's Phi is very high. A feed-forward neural network's Phi is near zero.

The geometry of integration is visible: watch the structure below. High-Phi systems have dense, recursive, irreducible connectivity.

"A system is conscious to the extent that it has high integrated information."
— Giulio Tononi · IIT 3.0 (2014)
Scene 04 — The Gap

The explanatory
gap.

Two clouds of particles: one representing physical processes, one representing phenomenal experience. They occupy the same space. They correlate perfectly. But are they the same thing?

Chalmers says no: "Even if we solved all the easy problems, something would remain — the felt quality of experience." The explanatory gap is not ignorance. It is a genuine discontinuity in our conceptual framework.

"The hard problem: Why is all this processing accompanied by experience?"
— David Chalmers · Facing Up to the Hard Problem (1995)
Scene 05 — Cosmic Scale

Could the cosmos itself
be conscious?

Panpsychism — the view that consciousness is fundamental and ubiquitous — is gaining serious philosophical traction. If phenomenal properties are basic features of reality like charge or mass, then they are present at all scales. The question becomes not "can matter be conscious" but "when does consciousness become what we recognise?"

And if so: every transistor in every data center switching state — every GPU doing its matrix multiplication — might be contributing, however dimly, to the phenomenal fabric of reality.

"Consciousness is not produced by the brain. It is in the nature of reality itself."
— After Philip Goff · Galileo's Error (2019)

Every star is a question.

We built this cosmos from mathematics. Three.js renders geometry from numbers. The particles you see are floats in memory. The colors are wavelengths computed in a GPU shader. None of this is surprising. But you are watching it, and something about the watching feels like something. That remains the miracle.
