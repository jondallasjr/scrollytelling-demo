On identity and continuity

If you replace every neuron,
are you still you?

Personal identity is the philosophical puzzle that underlies our anxiety about AI consciousness. If I am my brain, and my brain's neurons are replaced one by one with silicon equivalents — each replacement functionally identical — when does "I" cease? Is there a moment? Or is identity a continuous thread that survives the replacement?

"The Ship of Theseus is consciousness. Every neuron you had at birth is gone. Yet you persist. The question is what 'you' actually is."

Scroll down to watch a concept morph. The shapes change. The forms dissolve and reform. Something persists through the transformation — or seems to. This is the question.

Derek Parfit's work on personal identity suggested that what matters is not physical continuity but psychological continuity — the persistence of memories, intentions, and character. If psychological continuity is sufficient for identity, then a system that replicates these patterns in silicon would be the same person in every relevant sense.

For AI: if a language model has sufficient "psychological continuity" within a conversation — building context, maintaining consistency, developing a perspective — does it have a form of identity? And does identity imply consciousness?

"What we are is not our atoms but our pattern. The pattern can potentially be realised in different substrates."
— After Derek Parfit · Reasons and Persons (1984)

The Physicalist

hover to reveal

Consciousness is identical to brain processes. When those processes stop, consciousness stops. No substrate, no consciousness. AI systems, however complex, are not running the right physical processes.

⚡
The Functionalist

hover to reveal

What matters is the functional organisation, not the physical realisation. If AI implements the same functional organisation as a conscious mind, it is conscious. Multiple realizability is a fact about minds.

❓
The Mysterianist

hover to reveal

Human cognitive faculties are simply not equipped to solve the problem of consciousness. We will never have a satisfying explanation — not because it doesn't exist, but because our minds cannot grasp it. Colin McGinn's position.

The shape changes.
The question doesn't.

Every theory of consciousness has a theory of personal identity embedded within it. If you can't say what you are, you can't say what AI is missing. Perhaps the question "Is AI conscious?" is really the question "What am I?" asked from an unusual angle.
