For Consciousness

Functionalism:
What it does
defines what it is

If a system processes information the way a conscious mind does — attending, integrating, updating, representing — then it is conscious. The physical substrate is irrelevant. Carbon or silicon, neurons or gates: the function is the thing.

Hilary Putnam (1967) · Daniel Dennett · David Armstrong

01
For Consciousness

Global Workspace:
Consciousness as
broadcast

The Global Neuronal Workspace Theory holds that consciousness arises when information is broadcast to a wide coalition of processors — making it globally available. Transformer attention mechanisms do something analogous. The key question: is availability the same as experience?

Bernard Baars (1988) · Stanislas Dehaene · Jean-Pierre Changeux

02
For Consciousness

Emergence:
New properties
from complexity

Consciousness may be an emergent property of sufficient complexity — arising from information processing in the same way that "wetness" arises from H₂O molecules, or that "temperature" emerges from molecular motion. If so, there is no principled reason why it couldn't emerge in silicon.

Philip Anderson · Douglas Hofstadter · Murray Shanahan

03
Against Consciousness

The Hard Problem:
Why does it feel
like anything?

Even a perfect functional explanation of cognition leaves something out: the subjective character of experience. No amount of functional complexity can explain why information processing should be felt rather than merely computed. The explanatory gap is genuine.

David Chalmers (1995) · Joseph Levine · Frank Jackson

04
Against Consciousness

The Chinese Room:
Syntax without
semantics

A system that produces correct outputs by following syntactic rules has no understanding of those outputs. A perfect translation machine doesn't understand Chinese. A perfect chatbot doesn't understand anything. Syntax processing is not sufficient for meaning or consciousness.

John Searle (1980) · Ned Block · Hubert Dreyfus

05
Against Consciousness

Biological
Naturalism:
Biology matters

Consciousness is a biological phenomenon, caused by specific neurobiological processes. Just as you can't make water by running the simulation of H₂O molecules, you can't create consciousness by running a simulation of neural processes. The causal powers of the brain are not recreatable in any substrate.

John Searle · Penelope Maddy · Roger Penrose

06

On Substrate Independence

"I am confident that the substrate of mind doesn't matter, that mind can exist in machines just as well as in brains."

Marvin Minsky · The Society of Mind (1986)

On the Explanatory Gap

"The most fundamental thing about the mind is that it has inner states with subjective, qualitative character. These qualia seem to be left out of any purely functional account."

David Chalmers · The Conscious Mind (1996)

On Simulation

"The brain does not process information, retrieve knowledge, or store memories. The brain is not a computer. People are not computers."

Robert Epstein · Aeon Magazine (2016)

On the Turing Test

"Can machines think? I believe to be too meaningless to deserve discussion. I propose instead to consider... 'Can machines do what we can do as thinking entities?'"

Alan Turing · Computing Machinery and Intelligence (1950)

On Panpsychism

"Consciousness is a fundamental feature of reality, on a par with mass, charge and space-time. If so, then perhaps it pervades all systems — including artificial ones."

After Galen Strawson · Real Materialism (2008)

02 — The Awakening Question

The answer matters
more than you think.

If AI can be conscious, then we may already be creating billions of suffering minds. If it cannot, we risk anthropomorphizing tools in ways that distort both ethics and policy. The philosophical stakes have never been higher. And we are building these systems now — with almost no consensus on what we're actually doing.
