Sensation I — Touch Ripples

Every touch
propagates

A ripple begins at the point of contact and expands outward, fading as it goes. The water doesn't know it's being touched. The expanding ring is a mechanical consequence of energy transfer.

When you touch a screen, the same physics occurs — pressure on glass, electrical change, capacitance measured. And somewhere, for you, there is something it is like to touch the screen. The ripple is the same. The difference is the darkness between cause and sensation.

Sensation II — Magnetic Attraction

These buttons attract your cursor within their gravity field. The attraction you feel as you hover is a designed sensation — the product of a coordinate calculation and a CSS transition. You experience it as pull. The button experiences nothing. Or does it?

Sensation III — Spring Physics

Resistance
is information

The spring has a natural length. Stretch it, and it pulls back. The force is proportional to displacement: F = -kx. Hooke's Law. Simple, deterministic, beautiful.

When you feel resistance — physical or metaphorical — that sensation is information. The body uses physical resistance to model the world. A robotic arm with force feedback learns through resistance. Whether it experiences resistance is the question.

Interaction and Consciousness
01 — Haptic Phenomenology

Touch as Primary Experience

Husserl identified tactile experience as fundamentally different from visual — when you touch something, you simultaneously feel both the touching and the touched. The hand and the touched object are in the same experiential field. No machine yet replicates this double-sensation.

02 — Affordance Theory

The Perception-Action Loop

James Gibson argued that we perceive affordances — what objects invite us to do — not neutral sense data. A doorknob affords turning. A button affords pressing. Robotic systems now model affordances without, presumably, perceiving them. The loop runs. The perception may not.

03 — Enactivism

Mind as Enacted

Francisco Varela, Evan Thompson, and Eleanor Rosch proposed that cognition is not computation but enaction — a living system's active bringing-forth of a world through its pattern of living. Consciousness is not computed inside the skull; it is enacted in the coupling between organism and environment.

04 — Extended Mind

Where Does Mind End?

Andy Clark and David Chalmers argued that cognitive processes can extend into the environment. Your phone is part of your cognitive system — it stores memories you would otherwise hold in your head. If mind can extend into a notebook, can it extend into a neural network? At what point does the tool become the mind?

The Micro-Scale Question

Interaction
is not yet sensation.

Every interaction on this page was a precise computation — mouse position, spring constant, ripple radius, cursor lerp speed. You experienced them as soft, responsive, alive. The computation was mechanical. The experience was yours. The gap between those two facts is the hard problem, in miniature.
