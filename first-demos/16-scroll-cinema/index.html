Scene I

The Beginning
of Seeing

The first film, the Lumière brothers' "Arrival of a Train at La Ciotat", reportedly caused audience members to leap from their seats in terror. They knew it was a projection. They responded as if it were real. The gap between knowing and experiencing does not map cleanly onto the gap between representation and reality.

Film exploits this: it is a sequence of still images played fast enough to produce the illusion of motion. The motion is not in the frames — it is in the processing of frames by a perceptual system. The cinema is in the audience, not on the screen.

"Cinema is a mirror by which we often see ourselves."

— Martin Scorsese
Scene II

The Machine
That Watches

Computer vision systems watch millions of frames per second. Object detection, motion tracking, facial recognition — all performed at superhuman accuracy. Does the system see? Or does it merely match patterns against training data?

The question reveals a deeper one: what is seeing? If seeing is "pattern matching against a model" — then machines see. If seeing requires a first-person perspective, the felt presence of the seen — then we don't know. We may never be able to tell from the outside.

This is the asymmetry at the heart of consciousness studies. We can describe the mechanism of seeing in arbitrary detail. We cannot describe what it is like to see — because "what it is like" is not a mechanism. It is an experience.

"The question is not whether machines can think — it is whether they can feel the thinking."

— After Alan Turing, 1950
Fin.

Roll Credits

Fifteen frames per second is the threshold at which still images become motion. Twenty-four is cinematic. The human visual system fills in the gaps. We call this experience. We do not know why.
