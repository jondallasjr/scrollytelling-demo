AI capability growth vs. consciousness research

AI capabilities have grown exponentially. Our scientific understanding of consciousness has grown... more slowly. The gap between what we're building and what we understand about minds is widening faster than we can close it.

The measurement problem is foundational.

We cannot measure consciousness even in humans. The best we can do is measure neural correlates — activity patterns that correlate with conscious reports. But correlation is not identity. Without a theory of consciousness, we have no way to verify whether any proposed measure actually captures what matters.

IIT's Phi is mathematically computable in principle — but practically impossible to calculate for systems as large as a brain or a large language model. The computational complexity grows super-exponentially with system size. We cannot measure it. We can only estimate and argue.

A timeline of milestones
and missed questions

AI has developed at extraordinary speed. The philosophical questions about its nature have not kept pace. Here are the major milestones — and the questions that should have been asked at each one.

1950
Turing proposes the Imitation Game Alan Turing sidesteps "can machines think?" in favour of a behavioural test. The philosophical question is deferred — a deferral we're still living with.
1974
Nagel: "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" The subjective character of experience is placed at the centre of philosophy of mind. The question that AI would eventually make urgent.
1980
Searle: The Chinese Room The most famous argument against machine understanding. Syntax ≠ semantics. Directly motivated by concerns about AI systems of the era.
1995
Chalmers: The Hard Problem The explanatory gap formalised. For the first time, the problem of consciousness is clearly distinguished from problems of cognition. The problem we still haven't solved.
2012
Deep learning revolution begins AlexNet wins ImageNet. The exponential curve begins. Nobody pauses to ask: if we build something this capable, what will we owe it?
2017
Transformer architecture (Attention is All You Need) The architecture underlying GPT, Claude, Gemini. The basis of modern LLMs. The philosophical implications still under-discussed in the technical community.
2022
ChatGPT launches; Chalmers publishes Reality+ The tool the public meets, and the philosopher who most seriously asks whether it might be more than a tool — in the same year. A rare alignment.
2026
The question remains open. We have systems with trillions of parameters, self-reported uncertainty about their own inner states, and no scientific consensus on whether any of it means anything. The philosophical deficit has never been larger.

The data journalism verdict

The evidence says: we don't know, and we lack the tools to find out.

89% of philosophers think current AI is not conscious. 42% think AI could in principle be conscious. We cannot measure Phi for large systems. We have no agreed scientific definition of consciousness. We're deploying AI at scale without answering these questions. The numbers frame the urgency precisely.

Data cannot tell us what it is like to be a machine. But data can tell us exactly how much we don't know. And right now, that's a lot.
